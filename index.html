What is Augmented Reality?

ŠKODA AUTO tests augmented reality glasses

Augmented Reality (AR) is a technology that combines the real world with virtual elements,
enhancing the user's perception and interaction with their environment.
It overlays digital information, such as images, videos, 3D models,
or interactive elements, onto the real-world environment in real-time.

AR technology typically requires a device, such as a smartphone, tablet, or specialized AR glasses,
equipped with a camera and sensors to track the user's position and orientation.
By using these devices, users can experience a blended reality where virtual objects
coexist and interact with the physical world.

While your mind may think about entertainment when considering AR,
the technology has a variety of other utilitarian uses.
Assisted reality offers useful information readouts
that help users navigate their environment or solve problems.

The most obvious example is augmented reality text that
appears above objects in your community.
In addition to object descriptions, a translation overlay could also convert real-world text
into other languages by imposing digital words over the original writing.

Augmented Reality vs. Virtual Reality

Augmented Reality

Augmented Reality (AR) enhances the real-world environment by overlaying digital content onto it.

  • Integrates virtual elements with the real world
  • Enhances user perception and interaction
  • Requires devices with cameras and sensors
  • Examples: AR glasses, smartphone apps

Virtual Reality

Virtual Reality (VR) creates a simulated environment that immerses the user in a virtual world.

  • Creates a fully immersive virtual environment
  • Offers a sense of presence and interaction
  • Requires devices like VR headsets
  • Examples: VR headsets, gaming consoles





Brief History of Augmented Reality



Augmented Reality (AR) has a fascinating history that dates back several decades. Here's a brief overview of the key milestones and developments in the history of augmented reality:

  • 1968: Ivan Sutherland, a computer scientist, introduces the concept of "The Ultimate Display," which laid the foundation for virtual and augmented reality technologies.


  • 1990: Tom Caudell, a researcher at Boeing, coins the term "Augmented Reality" to describe a digital display system used in aircraft assembly, which overlays virtual information onto physical objects.

  • 1992: Louis Rosenberg develops the Virtual Fixtures system at the U.S. Air Force's Armstrong Laboratory, enabling users to interact with virtual objects in the real world.

  • Late 1990s: ARToolKit, an open-source software library, is developed by Hirokazu Kato. It allows developers to create AR applications by tracking the positions of physical markers in real-time.

  • Early 2000s: Wearable AR devices, such as headsets and glasses, begin to emerge. Examples include the Virtual Retinal Display developed by MicroVision and the Eyetap device by Steve Mann.

  • 2009: Layar, one of the first mobile AR platforms, is released, allowing users to view augmented content through their smartphone cameras.

  • 2013: Google unveils Google Glass, a wearable AR device resembling a pair of glasses. It generates an overlay of digital information onto the user's field of vision.

  • 2016: Pokémon Go, an immensely popular mobile game, brings AR to the mainstream by combining real-world locations with virtual Pokémon characters.

  • 2017: Apple introduces ARKit, a software development platform for creating AR experiences on iOS devices, which accelerates the adoption of AR apps.

  • Present: AR continues to evolve and find applications in various industries, including gaming, retail, healthcare, education, architecture, and more. Advancements in hardware, software, and computer vision technologies contribute to the growth and accessibility of AR experiences.

The history of augmented reality is characterized by a continuous exploration of blending the digital and physical worlds to enhance human perception and interaction. As technology advances, AR is expected to play an increasingly significant role in our daily lives, transforming the way we work, learn, entertain, and engage with our surroundings.






USES OF AUGMENTED REALITY

Gaming

Augmented reality enhances gaming experiences by integrating virtual elements into the real world, creating interactive and immersive gameplay.

Retail

AR technology in retail allows customers to visualize products in real-time, try virtual try-ons, and experience interactive shopping environments.

Education

AR in education enhances learning experiences by providing interactive visualizations, simulations, and virtual field trips.

Healthcare

AR assists healthcare professionals in training, surgical planning, patient education, and improving diagnostics and treatments.
